## 1. Introduction

India, as a developing economy focused on sustainable development, needs all sections of society to have equal opportunity to participate in nation-building. Lack of digital financial literacy — especially among the rural population — is a major challenge, particularly in the context of demonetization and the push toward a cashless economy. There is an urgent need to create awareness of digital finance services in rural and semi-urban areas, and to support access to them.

**Project aim:** Enrol and impart digital financial literacy to **25 lakh merchants** and **1 crore citizens**, with the help of **2,00,000 CSCs** across all **2,50,000 panchayats** in the country.

**Objective:** Enable CSCs to become **Digital Financial Hubs** by:
- Hosting awareness sessions on government policies and digital finance options for rural citizens
- Supporting the use of digital finance mechanisms (IMPS, UPI, Bank PoS machines, etc.) by various stakeholders
- Enabling citizens to access and use electronic payment systems (EPS)
- Sensitizing and enabling merchants at the panchayat level to use EPS
- Creating broader awareness in rural India

**Steps to achieve Digital Financial Inclusion:** Sensitize → Enable → Access

**Implementation agency:** CSC e-Governance Services India Ltd. — a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) set up by the Government of India, with the infrastructure and network capability to run this initiative nationwide.

## 2. Common Service Centres (CSCs): An Introduction

**Common Service Centres (CSCs)** are access points delivering e-governance and business services to citizens in remote, rural, and semi-urban areas — a pan-India network catering to the country's regional, geographic, linguistic, and cultural diversity, supporting a socially, financially, and digitally inclusive society.

CSCs function as **last-mile delivery points** of government and public services in rural/remote areas. Given their spread and ICT infrastructure, CSCs are mandated to spread financial inclusion through services such as:
- Opening of bank accounts
- Banking facilitation
- Disbursing MGNREGS and other social security scheme entitlements
- Insurance and pension services

CSCs are thus positioned to bring the rural population into the formal financial system by providing awareness of, and access to, Digital Financial Services.

### CSC-SPV Nationwide Activities

- Organize workshops for awareness
- CSC interaction with communities
- Involve local leaders/opinion makers
- Sessions in colleges/schools
- Information drives in market haats
- Coverage of semi-urban municipalities
- Organize local-level marathons
- Competitions
- Radio/TV talk shows

### Stakeholders Involved

- **Government:** State Govt./District Administration, NIC-SIO/DIO, Banks, Panchayats
- **Agencies:** Merchant Associations, Market Haats, Merchants/Traders
- **Communities:** Semi-urban/rural population, Schools/Colleges, women, farmers, marginalized sections

## 4. Digital Financial Services

**Digital financial inclusion** refers to digital access to, and use of, formal financial services by excluded and marginalized sections of society. **Digital Financial Services (DFS)** are designed to suit customer needs and are delivered responsibly at a cost that's affordable for customers and sustainable for providers.

**Three key components of DFS:**
1. A digital transactional platform
2. Retail agents
3. Use of a device (usually a mobile phone) by customers and agents to transact via the platform

DFS is a means by which the unbanked population increasingly gains access to financial services through digital channels — banks, microfinance institutions, mobile operators, and third-party providers leverage mobile phones, point-of-sale devices, and networks of small-scale agents to offer basic financial services with greater convenience, scale, and lower cost than traditional banking.

**Major types of DFS covered in this handbook:**
1. Cards
2. USSD
3. AEPS
4. UPI
5. e-Wallet

## 4.1. Cards

*Campaign tagline: "My Mobile... My Bank... My Wallet... Transactions without Cash — It's Possible! Cards, PoS"*

### 4.1.1. What are cards?

Cards are usually issued by banks and classified based on their issuance, usage, and payment method. There are **three types**: Debit cards, Credit cards, and Prepaid cards.

### 4.1.2. Types of Cards

| Card Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Prepaid Cards** | Pre-loaded from a customer's bank account; usable for a limited transaction amount; rechargeable like a mobile top-up; safe to use. |
| **Debit Cards** | Issued by the bank where the account is held; linked directly to the account (current/savings/overdraft); expenditure is immediately debited; usable to withdraw cash up to the account balance; usable only for domestic fund transfers. |
| **Credit Cards** | Issued by banks/RBI-approved entities; usable domestically and internationally (if enabled); unlike debit cards, allows withdrawal beyond the account balance up to a set credit limit, with a repayment time limit — interest is charged on delayed repayment. |

### 4.1.3. How to Use Debit/Credit Cards

1. **At an ATM:** Insert the card, enter the 4-digit PIN provided by the bank. The bank sets a maximum daily withdrawal limit.
2. **Other ATM uses (debit cards):** Check balance, deposit cheque/money, get a mini-statement — without visiting a branch.
3. **At a retail store (Point of Sale process):**

   User gives the debit card to the cashier → Merchant runs the card through the PoS machine → Merchant enters the purchase amount → Consumer enters the card PIN → Bank debits the purchase amount directly from the user's account → Machine prints a receipt, signed by the user → Merchant keeps the signed receipt and hands the user a copy for records → Customer receives an SMS confirming the completed transaction.

### 4.1.4. Why Use These Cards?

- Shop anywhere with your card
- Use at shops, ATMs, wallets, Micro ATMs, online shopping
- Withdraw cash at ATMs, purchase goods/services at PoS, and shop online
- Pay all kinds of utility bills
- Book tickets (airline/railway/bus), hotels, and pay at restaurants
- Pay for any service at any location with a card reader/PoS machine

### 4.1.5. How to Get a Card

- Consumers can apply for Debit/RuPay/Credit cards at all Public and Private sector banks.
- Citizens can get a debit card by submitting an application to their bank branch.
- Citizens can exchange existing debit cards for a RuPay Card.
- Citizens without a bank account must first open one to get a card.
- Per Government orders, all Jan Dhan account holders are issued RuPay Cards.

## 4.2. USSD (Unstructured Supplementary Service Data)

*Campaign tagline: "It is as easy as checking your prepaid balance from your phone! It is possible to transact even on a normal mobile phone."*

### 4.2.1. What is USSD?

USSD brings banking services to every citizen via a **single number**, irrespective of telecom provider, mobile handset make, or region — offered through the **National Unified USSD Platform (NUUP)** on short code **\*99#**. Usable for payments up to **₹5,000 per day per customer**.

### 4.2.2. What You Need

- An account in a bank
- Any mobile phone on a GSM network
- A mobile number registered with the bank account

### 4.2.3. How to Use It

1. Visit your branch to link your mobile number to your bank account (can also be done at an ATM or online).
2. You will receive a **Mobile Money Identifier (MMID)** and **Mobile PIN (MPIN)**.
3. Remember your MPIN.

### 4.2.4. Benefits of USSD

- Simple and easy to use
- Works on almost all GSM handsets
- No GPRS/internet data plan required
- Works even while on the move
- More secure than SMS

### 4.2.5. Steps to Use USSD

1. Link your mobile number to your bank account.
2. Dial **\*99#** from your phone.
3. Enter the first 3 letters of your bank name or the first 4 letters of the IFSC.
4. Choose the "Fund Transfer – MMID" option.
5. Enter the payee's mobile number and MMID.
6. Enter the amount and your MPIN, leave a space, and enter the last 4 digits of your account number.

### 4.2.6. Services Available

**Non-financial services:**
- **Balance Enquiry** — check available balance linked to the mobile number
- **Mini Statement** — generate a mini account statement
- **Know MMID** — retrieve the MMID allotted during mobile banking registration
- **Generate/Change M-PIN** — set or update the mobile PIN (password) used to authenticate transactions

**Financial services:**
- **Fund Transfer using Mobile No. and MMID** — transfer funds using the beneficiary's MMID and mobile number
- **Fund Transfer using IFSC and Account No.** — transfer funds using the beneficiary's IFSC code and account number

*(Refer to your bank's website for further instructions.)*

## 4.3. AEPS (Aadhaar Enabled Payment System)

*Campaign tagline: "Why rely on your bank, when you can bank on Aadhaar! Now link your Aadhaar card with your bank account."*

### 4.3.1. What is AEPS?

AEPS empowers a bank customer to use their **Aadhaar** as identity to access their Aadhaar-enabled bank account and perform basic banking transactions. It allows bank-to-bank transactions at a PoS (Micro ATM), with the help of a **Banking Correspondent (BC)/VLE**. The user's account must be seeded with their Aadhaar number (at the bank, or via BC/VLE). Transactions can be done at any AEPS point **without any PIN or password**.

### 4.3.2. What You Need to Activate AEPS

- **IIN** (Identifying the bank the customer is associated with)
- **Aadhaar Number**
- **Fingerprint** captured during enrolment

### 4.3.3. Transactions Possible with AEPS

- Balance Enquiry
- Cash Withdrawal
- Cash Deposit
- Aadhaar-to-Aadhaar Funds Transfer
- Purchase at Fair Price Shops with AEPS

### 4.3.4. Key Steps for an AEPS Transaction

1. Go to a MicroATM or Banking Correspondent.
2. Provide your bank name and Aadhaar number.
3. Choose which transaction to perform.
4. Provide your fingerprint on the scanner.
5. Take the printed slip after a successful transaction.

### 4.3.5. Why Use AEPS?

- Easy to use, safe, and secure — uses Aadhaar number and fingerprints
- Based on demographic and biometric/iris data, eliminating fraud and non-genuine activity
- Facilitates disbursement of government entitlements (NREGA, Social Security Pension, Handicapped Old Age Pension, etc.) via Aadhaar authentication
- Enables inter-operability across banks safely and securely
- Extends financial services to unreached clients beyond branch networks, especially in unbanked/under-banked areas
- No transaction charges (at present)
- No need to remember a bank account number
- Consumers with a biometric device can transact at home using a computer or Android phone/tablet (some tablets have built-in biometric devices)

## 4.4. UPI (Unified Payments Interface)

*Campaign tagline: "It's as easy as sending a message from your phone!"*

### 4.4.1. What is UPI?

UPI is a system for **instant, electronic payments** through a user's smartphone — an advanced version of the Immediate Payment Service (IMPS), which was used to transfer money between bank accounts. Like IMPS, UPI facilitates round-the-clock funds transfer.

- Authenticates the user's identity like a debit card does, using the phone as the tool
- Works **24×7, 365 days**

### 4.4.2. What You Need

- A smartphone with a UPI application (app)
- A bank account

### 4.4.3. How UPI Works — 3-Step Process

1. **Registration** — Download your bank's UPI app; register with your account details; create a virtual ID with your name or phone number. (One-time process.)
2. **Online Purchase** — Add the item to your cart; select "Payment through UPI" at checkout; provide only your Virtual ID to the merchant.
3. **Mobile Authentication** — Receive a pop-up on your phone's UPI app requesting approval; enter the secure PIN; receive confirmation of successful payment.

### 4.4.4. Features and Benefits

- Eliminates the need to share your name, bank account number, IFSC code, or bank branch
- Eliminates the use of physical tools like debit cards
- No need for multi-step, phishing-prone processes like net-banking
- Simple application usable by anyone
- Instant, secure authentication — can be initiated anywhere
- Opens the pathway to a completely cashless digital society
- Can be used to send money requests, like an invoice
- Usable for online payment of utility bills and school fees

## 4.5. e-Wallet

*Campaign tagline: "It's as easy as sending photos through your phone!"*

### 4.5.1. What is an e-Wallet?

An **e-wallet (electronic wallet)** is a type of electronic card used for online transactions via computer or smartphone, functioning like a credit or debit card. It must be linked to the individual's bank account to make payments. Its main objective is to make **paperless money transactions** easier.

### 4.5.2. Features of e-Wallet

- A type of prepaid account where a user can store money for future online transactions
- Protected with a password
- Enables payments for groceries, online purchases, flight tickets, etc.

### 4.5.3. How It Works

An e-wallet has two main components:
- **Software component** — stores personal information, provides security and encryption
- **Information component** — a database of user-provided details: name, shipping address, payment method, amount to be paid, card details, etc.

### 4.5.4. How to Use an e-Wallet

**For Consumers:**
1. Download the app on your device.
2. Sign up by entering relevant information; receive a password.
3. Load money via debit/credit card or net banking.
4. When shopping online, the e-wallet auto-fills the user's information on the payment form.
5. After payment, the user needn't re-fill order forms on other websites — information is stored and auto-updated.

**For Merchants:**
1. Download the app on your device.
2. Sign up by entering relevant information; receive a password.
3. Self-declare as a merchant.
4. Start accepting payments.

**What You Need to Start Using an e-Wallet:**
1. Bank Account
2. Smartphone
3. 2G/3G/4G connection
4. A free wallet app

**Must-Do Practices:**
- Register your mobile number with the bank for SMS alerts on every transaction
- Never share your PIN with anyone
- Transact only at trusted merchants
- At an ATM, ensure no one is looking over your shoulder

## 5. VLEs and Their Role in the Project

Trained **Village Level Entrepreneurs (VLEs)** identify and mobilize the community per these selection criteria:

1. One person from each household, selected in consultation with panchayat representatives
2. Each CSC covers 40 participants — one per household, across 40 households in the catchment area
3. Account activation for an average of 40 citizens per CSC
4. Each VLE covers an average of 10 merchants per panchayat, supporting them in accessing the digital payment platform and activating accounts
5. Awareness sessions at district and block level for government functionaries and others on digital financial platforms
6. VLEs collect evidence/records of citizens using the cashless facility after training
7. VLEs upload all project-related information to the online monitoring tool developed by CSC-SPV
8. VLEs use innovative outreach methods, including:
   - Visiting schools/colleges to engage students with the tools/technologies
   - Using NSS volunteers to create awareness
   - Mobilizing self-help groups and other voluntary organisations
   - Organizing marathons with local government agency support
   - Meeting with Merchant Associations/Traders/Hawkers to create awareness
   - Visiting market days to enable hawkers/vendors to adopt cashless transactions
   - Engaging with farmers and mandis

### Project Workflow

Mobilization of CSCs → Content customization → Development of IEC materials and OMA → Master Training of VLEs → Identification and mobilization of the community by VLEs → District/Block-level sessions → Registration of participants → Conduct of session → Impact assessment

## 5.1. Role of VLE Society

- The **VLE Society** helps make respective **districts** "Cashless."
- The **individual VLE** helps make **Panchayats/local areas** "Cashless."

## 5.2. Incentives

- VLEs and their societies are given awards for making a panchayat/society cashless.

## 5.3. Suggestions for VLEs

- Enable every trader/merchant/hawker to use/adopt digital financial technologies/tools — once transaction points support cashless transactions, citizen acceptance follows more easily.
- Encourage and motivate citizens to make payments only through digital tools.
- Use a comprehensive, area-based approach as a model/stimulus for others to adopt and accept.
