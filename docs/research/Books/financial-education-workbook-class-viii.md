## History — Introduction to Insurance

### Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Insurance** | A contract between the insurer and the policyholder; in return for a premium, the insurer promises to pay a specified amount on the happening of a specific event |
| **Insured** | The person or object covered under insurance |
| **Insurer** | The insurance company that receives the premium and compensates for financial losses |
| **Life Insurance** | Financial cover for contingencies linked to human life — death, disability, accident, retirement, etc. |
| **Nominee** | The person(s) nominated by the policyholder to receive policy benefits upon their death |
| **Policy Term** | The duration for which an insurance policy provides cover |
| **Sum Assured** | The amount the insurer is obliged to pay in the event of a covered loss or damage |

### History of Insurance in India

Insurance in India has deep roots, referenced in the writings of Manu (*Manusmrithi*), Yagnavalkya (*Dharmasastra*), and Kautilya (*Arthasastra*) — describing the pooling of resources for redistribution during calamities like fire, floods, epidemics, and famine. Early traces also appear in marine trade loans and carriers' contracts.

**Key milestones:**

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1818 | Oriental Life Insurance Company established in Calcutta — India's first life insurer (failed 1834) |
| 1829 | Madras Equitable begins life insurance business in the Madras Presidency |
| 1870 | British Insurance Act enacted |
| 1871–1897 | Bombay Mutual (1871), Oriental (1874), Empire of India (1897) established in Bombay |
| 1912 | Indian Life Assurance Companies Act — first statutory measure regulating life insurance |
| 1928 | Indian Insurance Companies Act — enabled government to collect statistics on life/non-life business |
| 1938 | Insurance Act, 1938 — consolidated and strengthened regulatory control |
| 1950 | Insurance Amendment Act abolishes Principal Agencies |
| 1956 | Life Insurance nationalized; Life Insurance Corporation (LIC) formed, absorbing 154 Indian and 16 non-Indian insurers plus 75 provident societies |
| 1850 | Triton Insurance Company Ltd. established in Calcutta — origin of general insurance in India |
| 1907 | Indian Mercantile Insurance Ltd. — first company to transact all classes of general insurance |
| 1957 | General Insurance Council formed; code of conduct framed |
| 1968 | Insurance Act amended to regulate investments and set minimum solvency margins; Tariff Advisory Committee set up |
| 1972–73 | General Insurance Business (Nationalisation) Act — 107 insurers amalgamated into 4 companies: National Insurance, New India Assurance, Oriental Insurance, United India Insurance; General Insurance Corporation of India (GIC) incorporated (1971), commenced business Jan 1973 |
| 1993–94 | Malhotra Committee (chaired by former RBI Governor R.N. Malhotra) recommends opening the sector to private and foreign players |
| 1999–2000 | Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) constituted as an autonomous statutory body (April 2000); market opened to registrations in August 2000; foreign ownership capped at 26% |
| 2000–2002 | GIC subsidiaries restructured as independent companies; GIC converted into a national re-insurer; four subsidiaries de-linked from GIC (2002) |
| By 2014 | 28 general insurance companies (incl. ECGC and Agriculture Insurance Corporation of India) and 24 life insurance companies operating in India |

*Fun fact box: IRDAI regulates the insurance industry in India.*

## History — Types of Insurance

### Background

Insurance is a mechanism of sharing the losses of a few across many, reducing the cost or effect of loss from various risks. It accumulates funds (invested to boost the economy) to compensate policyholders for losses, without preventing the loss event itself.

### Types of Insurance

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Life Insurance** | A contract where the insurer pays the nominee a sum upon the insured's death, in exchange for premiums. Protects family income against the loss of a breadwinner. |
| **Health Insurance** | Covers the cost of an insured individual's medical and surgical expenses. |
| **Travel Insurance** | Covers medical expenses, financial default, and other losses incurred while travelling domestically or internationally. |
| **Home Insurance** | Covers building and/or contents against damage from fire, bad weather, natural disaster, burglary, or attempted burglary. |
| **Motor Insurance** | Covers a vehicle against financial loss/liability from accident or theft. Third-Party Liability insurance is legally compulsory in India; driving without it is a punishable offence. |

### Case Studies

**Case Study I:** Satish dies in a car accident, leaving his wife financially devastated with a young child to raise. His friend Ashish, seeing this, decides to get insurance to protect his own family. *(Points to Life Insurance.)*

**Case Study II:** Roshni crashes her car; her insurance company pays for all repair charges. *(Points to Motor Insurance.)*

## Civics — Part I: Types of Banks

### What is a Bank?

Per the Banking Regulation Act, 1949, banking means accepting deposits of money from the public — for lending or investment — repayable on demand or otherwise, and withdrawable by cheque, draft, order, or otherwise.

### Scheduled vs. Non-Scheduled Banks

A **Scheduled Bank** is included in the Second Schedule of the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934, and must:
- Have paid-up capital and reserves of at least ₹5 lakh, assuring the RBI its operations don't harm depositor interests
- Be a corporation or co-operative society (not a partnership or single-owner firm)

Banks not meeting these conditions are **Non-Scheduled Banks**.

### Types of Banks (by function)

1. **Commercial Banks** — Both scheduled and non-scheduled, financing trade and commerce. Scheduled commercial banks are grouped into Nationalised banks, SBI & Associates, Private sector banks, Foreign banks, and Regional Rural Banks (RRBs).

2. **Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)** — Set up to develop the rural economy, providing credit to small/marginal farmers, agricultural labourers, artisans, and small entrepreneurs.

3. **Co-operative Banks** — Split into:
   - **Rural Co-operative Banks** — Short-term structure (State Co-operative Banks → District Central Co-operative Banks → Primary Agricultural Credit Societies) for production/marketing credit; Long-term structure (State Co-operative Agriculture and Rural Development Banks → Primary Co-operative Agriculture and Rural Development Banks) for long-term agricultural credit needs.
   - **Urban Co-operative Banks (UCBs)** — Serve middle/lower-income groups in urban and semi-urban areas.

4. **Reserve Bank of India** — The apex/central bank, controlling and supervising the monetary/credit system: currency management, banker to government and to banks, custodian of foreign reserves, lender of last resort, credit controller, and driver of development functions.

### Types of Banks (by ownership)

| Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **Public Sector Banks** | Owned/controlled by the government (nationalised banks, RRBs) |
| **Private Sector Banks** | Owned by private individuals or corporations |
| **Co-operative Banks** | Operated along co-operative lines, organised under co-operative societies law |

### Types of Banks (by domicile)

| Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **Domestic Banks** | Registered and incorporated within the country |
| **Foreign Banks** | Foreign in origin, head-quartered abroad |

## Civics — Part II: Cheque

A **cheque** is a document that orders payment of money from a bank account. Information printed on a cheque includes: personal information (name), bank information (name, address, city, state, PIN), the **cheque number** (sequential), the **MICR number**, the **account number**, and an indicator of current vs. savings account.

### What is MICR Code?

**Magnetic Ink Character Recognition (MICR)** is a character-recognition technology used by banks to process cheques, printed in magnetic ink (usually containing iron oxide) and read by machine (also legible to humans). It appears on the bottom strip of the cheque and is used by the RBI's clearing process to identify the branch and bank.

MICR comprises **9 digits**, sub-divided as:
1. **City code** — first 3 digits
2. **Bank code** — next 3 digits
3. **Branch code** — last 3 digits

### What is IFSC?

The **Indian Financial System Code (IFSC)** is an **11-character code** identifying the bank and branch where an account is held, used for both NEFT and RTGS fund transfers.

### Types of Cheques

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Bearer Cheque** | Payable to whoever presents it (the words "or bearer" are not cancelled); risky if lost, as the finder can collect payment |
| **Order Cheque** | Payable to a specific named person, without transfer-prohibiting words |
| **Open/Uncrossed Cheque** | Not crossed; payable at the bank counter; may be bearer or order |
| **Crossed Cheque** | Two parallel lines drawn across the face (with/without "& CO." or "Account Payee" or "Not Negotiable"); cannot be cashed at the counter, only credited to the payee's account |
| **Ante-Dated Cheque** | Bears a date earlier than presentation date; valid for 3 months from date of issue |
| **Post-Dated Cheque** | Bears a future date; cannot be honoured before that date |
| **Stale Cheque** | Presented more than 3 months after its date; not honoured by the bank |

## Civics — Tax

### Types of Tax

| Tax | Description |
|---|---|
| **Income Tax** | Government levy on individuals/entities, varying with income or profits (taxable income) |
| **Property Tax ("House Tax")** | Tax on buildings and land, imposed on owners by the local government |
| **Goods and Services Tax (GST)** | A single, comprehensive, multi-stage, destination-based indirect tax on the supply of goods and services |

### Goods and Services Tax (GST)

The GST Act was passed in the Lok Sabha on **29th March 2017** and came into effect from **1st July 2017**. It subsumed several state taxes, including: State VAT, Central Sales Tax, Purchase Tax, Luxury Tax, Entry Tax (all forms), Entertainment Tax (except that levied by local bodies), taxes on advertisements, taxes on lotteries/betting/gambling, and related state cesses/surcharges.

**Meaning of GST:** GST is one indirect tax for the whole nation, making India a unified common market. It is a single tax on the supply of goods and services, from manufacturer to consumer, with the final consumer bearing only the GST charged by the last dealer, and set-off benefits available at all previous stages.

**Multi-stage nature:** GST is levied at every point of sale as an item moves through the supply chain — purchase of raw materials → production/manufacture → warehousing of finished goods → sale to retailer → sale to end consumer.

**Value Addition:** Tax is applied at each stage on the monetary value added — e.g., a manufacturer weaving yarn into a shirt adds value; a warehousing agent adding labels/tags adds further value; a retailer packaging and marketing the shirt adds even more.

**Destination-Based:** GST revenue accrues to the state where the goods/services are finally consumed, not where they are produced. (Example: goods manufactured in Rajasthan but sold to a consumer in Karnataka — the tax revenue goes to Karnataka.)

**GST Rate Structure:** 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28%, with a special rate of 0.25% on rough precious/semi-precious stones and 3% on gold. An additional cess of 15% (or other rates) applies on top of 28% GST for items like aerated drinks, luxury cars, and tobacco products.

**Excluded from GST:** Alcohol for human consumption (excluded by constitutional definition). Five petroleum products — crude oil, petrol, high-speed diesel, natural gas, and aviation turbine fuel — were temporarily kept out, pending a GST Council decision. Electricity is also kept out of GST.

**GST Administration in India:** Reflecting India's federal structure, there are three components:
- **CGST** (Central GST) — levied and collected by the Centre
- **SGST/UTGST** (State/Union Territory GST) — levied and collected by States/UTs on intra-state transactions
- **IGST** (Integrated GST) — levied and administered by the Centre on inter-state supply, being the aggregate of CGST and SGST of the destination state

### Why Do We Pay Taxes?

Taxation is the government's primary source of revenue, funding national development — roads, infrastructure, educational institutions, and more. While taxpayers may not see a direct individual benefit, the money serves the public collectively. Taxes also help redistribute resources across individuals and classes of the population, though they reduce personal disposable income available for goods, services, savings, and investment.

## Geography — Part II: Demand Draft (DD) / Payment Order

A **Demand Draft (DD)** is an instrument used to make payments or transfer money, issued by one branch of a bank on another branch of the same bank. It can be prepared via cash payment or by debiting a bank account.

- **Validity:** DDs are valid for **6 months**.
- **Cost:** DD amount + Commission + Service Tax.
- **Cash-purchased DD cap:** The total amount (inclusive of commission and taxes) must not exceed **₹49,999** when paid in cash.
- **Parties involved:** Drawer (the issuing bank) and Payee (the beneficiary).

**Why DDs are safer than cheques:** Since the drawer of a DD is a bank (not an individual), payment is certain and cannot be dishonoured for insufficient funds — unlike a personal cheque.

**Merits of using a DD:**
- Extremely unlikely to be stolen and misused
- No risk of counterfeit notes
- Can be encashed only by the named person
- Can be safely sent by courier/post
- Can be easily cancelled
- Sender can be easily identified

**Details required to send a DD:** Type of DD, mode of payment (account debit or cash), place of payment, recipient's name, and the amount.

### Payment Orders / Banker's Cheques

Similar to demand drafts but typically issued for **within-city** payments. Usually valid for **3 months**. Banks may charge a commission for these.

## English — How to Open a Bank Account

### General Introduction to Banking

Banks act as a **"middleman"** between depositors (who have surplus money) and borrowers (who need money) — individuals building/buying homes, farmers buying land/equipment, businesses expanding, students needing education loans. Banks pay interest to depositors as an incentive to keep money with them, and charge a higher interest rate to borrowers; the difference covers bank expenses (salaries, rent, electricity, etc.) and profit.

### Account Types (by account holder needs)

- **Savings Bank (SB) Account**
- **Current Account (CA)**
- **Recurring Deposit Account (RD)**
- **Fixed or Term Deposit (FD)**
- **Cumulative Term Deposit (CTD)**

### Documents Required to Open a Bank Account

| Requirement | Accepted Documents |
|---|---|
| **Address Proof** | Telephone bill, ration card, driving license, voter ID, passport, electricity bill, or Aadhaar card |
| **Identity Proof** | PAN card, passport, voter ID, or Aadhaar card |
| **Photographs** | 2 |
| **Minimum Balance** | Varies by bank/account type; No-Frills/Zero-balance accounts require none |

Additional requirements: a photo affixed to the form, a specimen signature card (usually scanned and stored digitally), and introduction by an existing account holder if KYC (Know Your Customer) norms aren't otherwise met.

### Children's Bank Accounts — General Features

- A guardian/parent can open an account for a minor — jointly held, or independent if the minor is **over 10 years old**
- Parent/guardian can receive regular bank statements and set up automatic transfers into the child's account
- Basic facilities available: debit card, passbook, and (at some banks) cheque books for minors
- Some banks offer additional insurance cover on the parent/guardian's life, with a sum assured to the minor
- On reaching majority, the account converts to a regular account, requiring fresh photographs and specimen signature

**Documents for a minor's account:** Proof of age and address, proof of relationship to the parent/guardian, proof of the minor's identity, and photographs.

**Typical minimum balance requirement:** ₹1,000 to ₹5,000, varying by bank.

## English — Insurance: A Story

### A Village Insured Against Nature's Vagaries

A village repeatedly suffers losses — crop loss from untimely rain, flooding that damages households/cattle/tractors, and a severe drought. The village's only graduate proposes **group insurance**: since the village's households form a homogeneous group (all part of the same Gram Panchayat), a single group policy covering homes, cycles, cattle, and crops is cheaper and administratively simpler than individual policies.

He also explains **cashless health insurance**: many insurers have tie-ups with hospital networks where claims are paid directly to the hospital, so patients only need to show their insurance ID to access care without upfront payment.

The village Mukhia (head) decides to insure the entire village under a group policy.

**Key takeaway:** Group insurance is often cheaper and simpler than individual insurance because it covers a homogeneous group under one policy, reducing administrative overhead — and cashless facilities remove the need for policyholders to pay out-of-pocket during hospitalisation.

### Deposit (Pay-in) Slip

To deposit money, a **pay-in slip / deposit slip** must be filled with the following details:

| # | Detail | Example Entry |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Name of the bank's branch | Jayanagar |
| 2 | Date of deposit | 03–04–06 |
| 3 | Paid in to the credit of (nature of account) | SB (Savings Bank) |
| 4 | Account holder's name | Ramesh K.N. |
| 5 | Amount of deposit in words | Two thousand only |
| 6 | Account number | 14502 |
| 7 | Amount of deposit in figures | 2000 |
| 8 | Signature of depositor | (Signature) |
| 9 | Denomination (break-up by currency note value) | 500×2 = 1000; 100×10 = 1000; Total = ₹2000 |
| 10 | Cash/Cheque (method of deposit) | Cash |

*Note: Since no authorization is required to deposit money, anyone can deposit into anyone's account.*

### Withdrawal Slip

To withdraw money using a withdrawal form, the following must be filled:

| # | Detail |
|---|---|
| 1 | Name of the account holder |
| 2 | Date of withdrawal |
| 3 | Account number in the bank |
| 4 | Amount of withdrawal in words |
| 5 | Amount of withdrawal in figures |
| 6 | Debit instruction to my/our SB A/c No. |
| 7 | Signature of depositor |

**Restrictions on withdrawal slips:**
1. Only the account holder can use it to withdraw for themselves.
2. Cannot be used to make payment to others.
3. The account holder must produce the passbook.

## Mathematics — Part II: Operating Your Account

### Depositing and Withdrawing Money

With a savings or current account, deposits and withdrawals can be made **in person at the bank** or via an **Automated Teller Machine (ATM)**.

- **Making a deposit:** A received cheque is deposited with the beneficiary's own bank, which collects the money through the **clearing house system** — banks in a city exchange cheques and settle net payables/receivables.
- **Making a withdrawal (via cheque):** Write a cheque made out to "Cash," date and sign it, then present it to the bank teller for cash.
- **Making a withdrawal (via ATM):** Insert the ATM card, enter the PIN, and specify the withdrawal amount.

### Cheque — Field-by-Field Breakdown

| # | Detail | Example Entry |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pay (name of the payee) | Ramesh K.N. |
| 2 | Date | 31–03–1999 |
| 3 | Rupees (amount in words) | One Thousand Only |
| 4 | Rs. (amount in figures) | 1000 |
| 5 | Signature of the issuer | (must match the specimen signature on file) |
| 6 | Account number | 11987 |
| 7 | Bank branch address | Public Utility Building Branch, Bangalore–560001 |
| 8 | Cheque number | 665078 |
| 9 | Mode of payment | A/c Payee |

**Key notes:**
- Overwriting is not permitted on a cheque.
- Do not write on or damage the MICR strip at the bottom of the cheque.

### Passbook

When a bank account is opened, the bank issues a **passbook** listing all transactions carried out on that account. If an account is held in two names (e.g., "Padmakshi and Veena"), it is called a **joint account**.

**Passbook transaction fields:**

| # | Field | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Date of transaction | The date money was deposited or withdrawn |
| 2 | Details/Particulars | How the money came in or went out (e.g., "By Cash," "To Agrahar & Co.") |
| 3 | Reference number | The bank's internal reference or the relevant cheque number |
| 4 | Amount withdrawn | Reduces the account balance |
| 5 | Amount deposited | Increases the account balance |
| 6 | Balance | The running balance in the account on that date |
