## Session 1 — Money Matters: They Really Matter!

### Activity I: Who is Wealthier?

Ravi and Mohan, students of Class 10 at ABC Public School, argue about who is wealthier and list what they each own:

**Ravi owns:**
- Cycle worth ₹2,250
- Watch worth ₹475
- Books worth ₹1,250
- A cricket bat worth ₹345 (he had borrowed a racquet from Rajesh)
- 5 Wonderland comics worth ₹35 each (2 of the 5 were borrowed from Arjun)

**Mohan owns:**
- Books worth ₹750
- Basketball worth ₹650
- A mobile worth ₹1,000
- Two borrowed game CDs from Chandan, worth ₹100 each
- A video game worth ₹1,250
- A tennis racquet worth ₹1,000 (lent to Pankaj for a few days)

> **Key concept:** Wealth is determined by **Net Worth**.
> **Net Worth = Assets − Liabilities**
> - **Asset:** Something you own
> - **Liability:** Something you owe

### Planning: The Mantra of Success!

Many goals go unfulfilled simply because we wish for them without a definite **plan**. A vague goal becomes a real one when it is made **SMART**:

- **S**pecific
- **M**easurable
- **A**chievable
- **R**ealistic
- **T**ime-Bound

**Example — Smart vs. Not Smart:**

| SMART Element | Not Smart | Smart |
|---|---|---|
| Specific | I want to go to picnic | I want to go to Matheran for picnic |
| Measurable | I will need some money to buy a cycle | I will need ₹2,000/- to buy a cycle |
| Achievable | I will win the cricket match alone | I will need the support of the team to win the match |
| Realistic | By winning the lottery I will buy a cycle | I will start saving to buy a cycle |
| Time-Bound | I will buy a cricket bat some time in future | I will save ₹50/- every month to buy a cricket bat in 10 months |

### Budgeting: Prioritizing the Needs!

**Activity IV — The Singh Family's Demands**

1. Shawl for Mr. Singh's mother (winter birthday gift)
2. Cycle for elder son Sahil
3. Latest video game for younger son Rohan
4. Wedding gift (gold set) for a common friend
5. Interior decoration of the house

**Prioritization matrix (Urgent × Important):**

| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | I. Urgent & important → Need to do now | II. Important but not urgent → Plan for later |
| **Not Important** | III. Urgent but not important → Do it if cash flows allow | IV. Neither urgent nor important → Ignore |

**Applied to the Singh family:**

| Demand | Urgent | Important | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shawl for Mr. Singh's mother | Y | Y | BUY |
| Sahil's cycle | N | Y | PLAN FOR LATER |
| Rohan's new video game | N | N | DON'T BUY |
| Marriage gift for common friend | Y | N | BUY A LESS EXPENSIVE GIFT, TO THE EXTENT CASH FLOWS PERMIT |
| House decoration | N | N | IGNORE |

> **Remember:** Needs are basic necessities one cannot wish away (e.g., a fan in summer). Wants make life comfortable but are not essential (e.g., an air conditioner).

### Budgeting Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Cash-flow Statement** | A record of your income and expenses |
| **Budget** | A plan for balancing income and expenditure, or a projection of one's income and expenditure |
| **Necessity of Budget** | Optimize savings; act as a "traffic signal" for spending; save accurately for goals; allot funds to expenditure areas in advance |
| **Budget Surplus** | Projected income > Projected expenditure |
| **Budget Deficit** | Projected income < Projected expenditure |
| **Postponing Satisfaction** | Giving up something now for something better/bigger later (e.g., skipping a video game to save for a bicycle) |
| **Instant Satisfaction** | Buying something the moment you want it |
| **Opportunity Cost** | The value of the next-best alternative given up when a choice is made (e.g., choosing a bicycle over a video game — the video game is the opportunity cost) |

## Session 3 — Investments: Keep Your Money Safe and Make It Work

### Once Upon a Time at a Bank

Ankush visits a bank with his father. The banker offers to not only return his ₹150 in savings but pay an additional ₹10 at year-end. This introduces the concept of **interest** — the return earned on money invested.

### The Three Pillars of Investment

1. **Safety** — Will your capital be returned?
2. **Liquidity** — Can you access your money when you need it?
3. **Growth** — What return (income or appreciation) will the investment generate?

### Activity IX: Piggy Bank vs. Real Bank

Compare a piggy bank and a real bank on Safety, Liquidity, and Growth. List at least three investment avenues.

### Banking Basics

A **bank** is an institution that accepts deposits and pays interest, then lends that money to borrowers at a higher rate of interest. The difference between the lending and borrowing rate is the bank's **Net Interest Margin (NIM)**.

**Types of Bank Accounts:**

| Account Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Savings Account** | For depositing surplus money and withdrawing as needed; usually requires a minimum balance; lowest interest rate |
| **Current Account** | For business transactions; no interest paid |
| **Term/Fixed Deposit** | Money deposited for a fixed, predetermined term; not needed immediately; higher interest rate than savings |

## Session 4 — Investment Mantras

### 1. Time Value of Money

**Sunny:** Invests ₹100/year from age 15 to 25 (10 years), then stops and never withdraws.
**Bobby:** Invests ₹400/year from age 25 to 35 (10 years), then never withdraws.

Both earn 15% p.a. *Question: Who accumulates more wealth by age 35, and why?* (The lesson underscores that starting early matters more than the amount invested, due to the extra years of compounding.)

### 2. The Eighth Wonder of the World — Compounding

**The Chessboard Story:** A king rewards the inventor of chess by doubling grains (or gold coins — *ashrafis*) on each of the 64 squares: 1, 2, 4, 8… By the 64th square, the total reaches **18,446,744,073,709,551,615** ashrafis — emptying the king's treasury. This illustrates the power of **exponential compounding**.

**Growth of ₹500 at various rates:**

| Years | 5% | 10% | 15% | 20% |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 525 | 550 | 575 | 600 |
| 5 | 638 | 805 | 1,006 | 1,244 |
| 10 | 814 | 1,297 | 2,023 | 3,096 |
| 15 | 1,039 | 2,089 | 4,069 | 7,704 |
| 25 | 1,693 | 5,417 | 16,459 | 47,698 |

### 3. Never Ever Give Up! — Harsha and Varsha

Twins Harsha and Varsha both invest ₹3,000/year starting at age 25, total invested ₹63,000 each by age 45, at 15% growth:

- **Harsha** stopped at 30, then invested lump sums (₹20,000 at 35, ₹25,000 at 42) → **₹4 lakh**
- **Varsha** continued investing small amounts regularly throughout → **₹3.8 lakh**

*Lesson: Regularity and duration of investment (time in the market) can outweigh lump-sum catch-up investing.*

### 4. The Magic of Number 72 — Rule of 72

To estimate how long it takes to double your money: **72 ÷ interest rate = years to double**.

Example: At 8% interest, money doubles in 72/8 = **9 years**. The rule also works in reverse — to find the rate needed to double money in a given number of years.

| Rate of Interest | Years to Double | Years to Invest | Rate Required to Double |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10% | 7.2 | 6 | 12% |
| 8% | 9 | 9 | 8% |
| 12% | 6 | 10 | 7.2% |

### 5. Skating Away — Leena's Story

Leena, 15, wants inline skates costing ₹40,000. Her skating teacher, who bought his own skates at 21 through years of small regular savings (₹150/month in high school, more later), helps her build a savings plan:

| Amount/Month | Period of Investment | Principal Amount | Possible Rate of Interest |
|---|---|---|---|
| ₹200 | 8 years | 19,200 | 10% |
| ₹300 | 6 years | 21,600 | 9% |
| ₹400 | 5 years | 24,000 | 8% |

*Lesson: Consistent saving and investing over time can fund even large future goals.*

### 6. The Inflation Termite: Beware!

**Inflation** is the phenomenon of rising prices over time. If a school bag cost ₹200 last year and ₹210 this year, the inflation rate is:

**(10/200) × 100 = 5%**

If savings aren't invested at a rate higher than inflation, purchasing power is eroded. Example: Investing ₹100 at 10% when inflation is 5% yields a **real rate of return** of only 5% (10% nominal − 5% inflation).

### Smart Guide for Family Financial Planning

- Identify sources of income
- Find out if there is a surplus or deficit
- Build a cash-flow statement and plan a budget
- Calculate savings that can be invested for returns
- Understand the benefits of investing

## Session 5 — Stock Market

### National Stock Exchange (NSE) and Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE)

BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange) is the oldest stock market in Asia and the largest in India by transaction volume (third largest globally). NSE (National Stock Exchange) and BSE are both Mumbai-based and together handle the vast majority of India's share transactions.

**What is a Stock Exchange?** An organization constituted to assist and carry out the buying, selling, or dealing in securities.

**What are Securities?** Financial instruments such as shares, bonds, and debentures issued by a company, body corporate, or government. A stock exchange provides the trading platform for these.

### About SEBI

The **Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)** was set up by Parliament in 1992 to protect investors and to regulate and develop the capital market. It began operations in 1988 via a Government of India order and is headquartered in Mumbai, with regional offices in Delhi, Kolkata, Chennai, and Ahmedabad.

**SEBI's role includes:**
- Requiring companies raising public capital to disclose full details and publish periodic information
- Protecting shareholder interests during company takeovers
- Licensing and regulating stockbrokers, sub-brokers, depositories, depository participants, portfolio managers, and merchant bankers
- Regulating mutual funds, including scheme disclosures and periodic reporting
- Investor education and grievance redressal, and introducing consumer-friendly disclosure norms

More information: [www.sebi.gov.in](https://www.sebi.gov.in)
