## Message 1: Loans without Collateral? Yes, It Is Possible!

**A short dialogue illustrates the idea:**

Raj, a small block-printing business owner, tells a friend his business has stalled because customers haven't paid him and he lacks money to buy dress material. His friend points out that if he doesn't do business, his profits will fall — to which Raj replies that banks would ask for guarantors and collateral, which he doesn't have.

His friend reassures him: he can ask his bank to provide a loan under the **CGTMSE Scheme**. Under this scheme, the bank will provide a loan without any collateral. Details are available at www.cgtmse.in and www.dcmsme.gov.in before visiting the bank.

### Credit Guarantee Trust (CGTMSE)

- A joint initiative of the Ministry of Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSME), Government of India, and the Small Industries Development Bank of India (SIDBI), with an objective to provide credit to the MSE sector without the need for collateral and third-party guarantee.
- The Credit Guarantee Fund Trust for Micro and Small Enterprises (CGTMSE) would provide cover facility of up to Rs. 200 lakhs.
- Guarantee and annual fees charged by CGTMSE are to be borne by the borrower or member lending institution.
- Instructions from the RBI to all banks: do not accept collateral security for loans up to Rs. 10 lakhs provided to the MSE sector.

Website: www.cgtmse.in

### General Credit Card (GCC)

Non-farm entrepreneurs can obtain credit facilities for both working capital and term loan requirements by getting a General Credit Card (GCC) from the bank.

A GCC is issued as a smart card/debit card (biometric smart card compatible for use in ATMs/handheld swipe machines and capable of storing adequate information of the entrepreneur's identity, assets, and credit profile, etc.). Wherever accounts are not digitized, the GCC may be issued as a card/passbook or a credit card-cum-passbook incorporating the name, address, photograph of the holder, particulars of borrowing limit, validity period, etc., which will serve as an identity card as well as facilitate recording of transactions on an ongoing basis.

The limit on the card will be fixed by the bank on a case-to-case basis based on a risk assessment. The rate of interest on the loans obtained through GCC will be decided by the bank. As an entrepreneur, you are expected to know how much the bank charges on your loan.

## Message 2: Loan Application Process

**Step 1:** Prepare your business plan and funds requirement estimate.
You can either visit the Udyami Mitra portal (https://udyamimitra.in/) or go to a bank directly.

**Step 2:** Get a loan application number and acknowledgment for the application.

**Step 3:** Respond to all queries from the bank/portal on the application promptly; wait for their decision.

**Step 4:** Track your application to check whether the loan has been sanctioned or rejected.

## Message 3: Timelines as Prescribed by BCSBI for Disposal of Loan Applications

The Banking Codes and Standards Board of India (BCSBI) has prescribed timelines for disposal of loan applications that are complete in all respects and are accompanied by documents as per the 'checklist' provided. Banks which are members of the BCSBI have to adhere to the following:

- MSE loan application for a credit limit or enhancement in the existing credit limit of up to Rs. 5 lakhs: **2 weeks**
- For credit limit above Rs. 5 lakhs and up to Rs. 25 lakhs: **3 weeks**

The Code does not replace or supersede regulatory or supervisory instructions issued by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), and banks will comply with such instructions/directions issued by the RBI from time to time.

## Message 4: Must-know Finance Terms for Entrepreneurs

The following scenarios use the example of a vegetable seller to illustrate key financial concepts.

### Scenario 1: Financing with Own Capital

A vegetable seller puts in his own capital of Rs. 2,000 and buys vegetables from a farmer. He pays Rs. 100 in rent per day and Rs. 50 in wages per day to a helper. He sells the vegetables for Rs. 2,500.

- The Rs. 100 in rent and Rs. 50 in wages paid are called **'Operating Expenses'**.
- The Rs. 2,000 spent on buying vegetables for selling is the **'Cost of Goods Sold'**.

**Profit = Selling Price – Cost of Goods Sold – Operating Expenses = 2,500 – 2,000 – 150 = Rs. 350**

### Scenario 2: Cash Credit against Hypothecation of Stock

Suppose the vegetable seller does not have his own capital to buy all the vegetables he requires, or he wants to expand his business. In such a scenario, he can take a loan from the bank by offering the vegetables to be purchased as security. This type of financing is called **cash credit**, where the bank lends against self-liquidating security.

The whole amount of the bank loan need not be drawn; only the amount needed based on current requirement. The interest on this type of facility is charged only on the outstanding, not on the entire loan limit.

### Scenario 3: Receivables Financing

In business, it is often necessary to provide credit, which means accepting payments from customers on a future date as per agreement. When that happens, there is not enough money to buy supplies for the next day and pay for other obligations like rent and wages. The payments to be received at a later date as per agreement are called **receivables**, and the customer who is expected to pay at a later date is called a **debtor**.

In such a situation, when money from sales is delayed, business need not stop for want of funds. The vegetable seller can go to the bank and request lending against receivables. The bank provides finance against the amount of receivables after reduction of margin. Interest on the loan is charged only on the outstanding amount of the loan.

### Scenario 4: Purchasing Fixed Assets and Accounting for Them

Now that profits have increased, the vegetable seller decides to buy a table and chair for the shop, costing Rs. 200.

He is in a dilemma: should he reduce this entire cost of Rs. 200 from this year's profit, even though he will use the furniture for the next ten years? Instead, since the furniture's life is ten years, he decides to allocate funds towards only 1/10th of the cost this year. The amount charged to the current year as expenses in this manner is called **depreciation**.

Items bought for running the business that will be used for more than one year are called **Fixed Assets** (examples: land, building, machinery, equipment, etc.). Depending on the life of the fixed asset, its value has to be reduced each year — this reduction is effected by setting apart the reduced amount as an expense, called depreciation.

### Scenario 5: Long-term Loans

As the business grows, the vegetable vendor realises that since vegetables are perishable, he had to throw away a fair amount not bought by customers, eating into his profits. He decides a cold storage system (freezer) may help increase the life of fresh vegetables and cut down on wastage. A good freezer costs about Rs. 20,000. He learns he can avail a loan through http://udayamimitra.in and applies. His loan is sanctioned at an interest rate of 10 per cent per annum. With the loan money, he buys a freezer, keeping vegetables fresh for an additional two days, leading to an increase in profits.

**Terminology — Term Loan:** Loans that are availed for a specific purpose and are to be repaid over a long period (one year and above) are called 'Term Loans'.

### Business Summary Example

The vegetable vendor's total sales figure was Rs. 10 lakhs, and the cost of goods sold was Rs. 8 lakhs — a gross profit of Rs. 2 lakhs. He paid Rs. 36,000 and Rs. 18,000 respectively on rent and wages (assuming 360 days in a year). His cash credit outstanding was Rs. 2,000 at year-end, generating Rs. 200 in interest. He repaid Rs. 6,000 towards the term loan on the last day of the year, leaving a term loan outstanding of Rs. 14,000, with Rs. 2,000 charged as interest for the year. Total interest paid for the year: Rs. 2,200. Depreciation of Rs. 2,000 (freezer) and Rs. 20 (furniture) totalled Rs. 2,020 (10% depreciation rate assuming a ten-year life for both). Fixed assets net of depreciation: Rs. 18,180 (Rs. 20,000 – Rs. 2,020).

## Message 5: Be Proactive and Approach the Bank in Case of Stress

If any of the following apply to your business:
- Your enterprise is in stress and you apprehend failure.
- You are not capable of paying your debts.
- There is erosion in net worth due to accumulated losses to the extent of 50% of your company's net worth during the previous accounting year.

...you can make an application to the bank branch or to the committee constituted at the district/divisional/regional level by the bank. The bank/committee will explore various options to resolve the stress in your account:

- **Rectification:** The bank/committee may obtain a commitment, specifying actions & timelines from you to regularize the account. They may also consider providing additional need-based finance.
- **Restructuring:** Consider the possibility of restructuring your account if it is viable and after ascertaining that you are not a wilful defaulter (i.e., no diversion of funds, fraud, or malfeasance).
- **Recovery:** If the bank/committee feels that the first two options are not viable, they may initiate recovery of the loan from your enterprise.

### Summary of Financial Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Profit & loss statement** | A statement showing income and expenses of the company. This is prepared for a particular period (usually 1 April to 31 March). |
| **Balance sheet** | A statement showing the position of assets and liabilities of the company as on a particular date. |
| **Revenue** | Amount received by way of sales of products and services. Includes both cash and credit sales. |
| **Cost of goods sold** | The direct material cost incurred in manufacturing or procuring a product that is sold to customers. |
| **Gross profit** | The difference between revenue and cost of goods sold. Does not include operating expenses like rent, salaries, etc. |
| **Operating expenses** | Expenses not directly associated with the production of goods, e.g., rent, salary, and SG&A (selling, general and administrative expenses). |
| **Interest** | Charges paid to the lender of money on the outstanding loan amount. |
| **Depreciation** | Annual charges applied to fixed assets depending on their life. Accounted as an expense in the profit and loss account. |
| **Profit before tax** | Gross Profit – Operating Expenses – Interest – Depreciation |
| **Assets** | Current assets such as cash, inventory, and non-current/fixed assets such as plant & machinery. |
| **Liabilities** | Shareholder capital, current liabilities such as creditors and short-term loans to be paid within one year, and term liabilities (to be paid after one year). |
| **Receivables** | Payments to be received from customers on a future date as per agreement. |
| **Current assets** | Cash, account receivables, and other liquid assets that can be readily converted to cash. |
| **Fixed assets** | Assets used for the long term and not sold during the normal course of business. |
| **Cash credit** | Working capital loans to finance current assets and manage day-to-day running of the business. |
| **Term loan** | A long-term loan generally taken to buy fixed assets, repaid in instalments over a period of time. |
| **Equity capital** | Shareholder's capital representing ownership interest in the company. |

*The definitions of the above terms are indicative in nature. They differ from one industry to another and are subject to interpretation.*

### Recent Initiatives for MSMEs

- **Trade Receivables Discounting System (TReDS):** Do you face the problem of delayed payments from your buyers? If yes, you can discount your receivables through TReDS. Three entities licensed by RBI — RXIL, Mynd Solution, and A.TReDS — operate the TReDS platform.
- **Certified Credit Counsellors:** Are you hesitant to apply to banks for MSME loans due to lack of knowledge on preparing business proposals, maintaining financial documents, etc.? You may approach Certified Credit Counsellors (CCCs) who are registered by SIDBI. A list of such counsellors is available at: https://udyamimitra.in/Home/CCC. These counsellors may assist you with business decisions.
